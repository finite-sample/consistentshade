\begin{abstract}
Neural networks exhibit substantial prediction variability when retrained on bootstrap samples of the same dataset, undermining reliability in deployment scenarios requiring consistent decision boundaries. We propose \emph{bootstrap-consistency regularization} (BCR), a training procedure that directly minimizes prediction variance across data resamples. Our method trains multiple shadow copies of a network simultaneously, each receiving Poisson-weighted losses that simulate bootstrap resampling, while penalizing disagreement between their predictions on identical inputs. We provide a first-order sensitivity analysis showing that bootstrap prediction variance depends on the gradient-Hessian-gradient product, which our variance penalty directly targets. Empirical evaluation on four tabular datasets demonstrates 22--50\% reductions in bootstrap prediction variance compared to standard training, with accuracy degradation typically under one percentage point. BCR provides strong stability improvements on classification tasks, though simpler methods (bagging, weight decay) may be equally effective on some regression tasks. Unlike existing approaches that target indirect proxies such as weight-space curvature, BCR directly optimizes the quantity of practical interest: prediction consistency under data resampling.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The deployment of neural networks in production systems requires not only predictive accuracy but also consistency across model updates. When a model is retrained on fresh samples from the same distribution, predictions on identical inputs should remain stable within the bounds justified by sampling uncertainty. However, neural networks commonly exhibit substantial \emph{refit variance}---the phenomenon whereby predictions vary significantly when models are trained on different bootstrap samples of the training data \citep{efron1979bootstrap}.

This instability poses significant challenges across multiple domains. In production machine learning systems, model updates may reverse binary classifications on borderline cases, creating inconsistent user experiences. Scientific applications require stable models for fair method comparisons and reliable bootstrap-based confidence intervals. Regulated industries face compliance issues when prediction variability across training runs triggers audit procedures.

Existing approaches to neural network stability primarily target indirect proxies for the desired behavior. Sharpness-Aware Minimization \citep{foret2020sharpness} and related methods penalize weight-space curvature under the assumption that flatter minima correspond to more stable predictions. Stochastic regularization techniques such as R-Drop \citep{liang2021rdrop} control prediction consistency under network noise but do not address data resampling variance. Teacher-student methods like Mean Teacher \citep{tarvainen2017mean} stabilize optimization dynamics while remaining agnostic to bootstrap variance. Standard bagging \citep{breiman1996bagging} reduces variance through ensembling but does not encourage individual models to be stable.

We propose \emph{bootstrap-consistency regularization} (BCR), a fundamentally different approach that directly minimizes prediction variance across data resamples. Our key contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Estimand}: We clearly define bootstrap prediction instability as the target quantity of interest---the variance of predictions across models trained on bootstrap resamples of the data.
    \item \textbf{Training objective}: We augment the standard empirical risk with an explicit instability penalty, creating a principled trade-off between accuracy and stability.
    \item \textbf{Algorithm}: We introduce a tractable stochastic surrogate that approximates full-retraining instability through mini-batch Poisson weighting, enabling efficient joint optimization.
    \item \textbf{Theory}: We provide first-order sensitivity analysis showing that bootstrap variance depends on gradient sensitivity through the loss Hessian, justifying why direct variance penalties target the right quantity.
    \item \textbf{Empirics}: We demonstrate that BCR reduces actual full-retraining instability by 22--50\% across four datasets, with competitive performance relative to natural baselines on the stability-accuracy Pareto frontier.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Method}

\subsection{Problem Formulation}

Consider a training dataset $\mathcal{D} = \{(x_i, y_i)\}_{i=1}^n$ drawn from distribution $P$, and a parameterized model $f_\theta: \mathcal{X} \rightarrow \mathcal{Y}$. For any test input $x \in \mathcal{X}$, we define the \emph{bootstrap prediction variance} as:

\begin{equation}
\sigma^2_{\text{boot}}(x) = \mathbb{E}_{\mathcal{D}' \sim \text{Boot}(\mathcal{D})}\left[(\hat{f}_{\mathcal{D}'}(x) - \mathbb{E}[\hat{f}_{\mathcal{D}'}(x)])^2\right]
\end{equation}

where $\hat{f}_{\mathcal{D}'}$ denotes the model obtained by training on bootstrap sample $\mathcal{D}'$, and $\text{Boot}(\mathcal{D})$ represents the bootstrap distribution over datasets of size $n$ sampled with replacement from $\mathcal{D}$.

Our objective combines standard empirical risk minimization with explicit bootstrap variance regularization:

\begin{equation}
\min_\theta \mathbb{E}_{(x,y) \sim P}[\ell(f_\theta(x), y)] + \lambda \cdot \mathbb{E}_{x \sim P_X}[\sigma^2_{\text{boot}}(x)]
\end{equation}

where $\ell$ is a loss function, $P_X$ is the marginal distribution of inputs, and $\lambda > 0$ controls the regularization strength.

\subsection{First-Order Sensitivity Analysis}

To understand what drives bootstrap prediction variance, we analyze the sensitivity of predictions to training data perturbations. Following the influence function framework \citep{koh2017understanding}, consider an infinitesimal upweighting of training point $(x_i, y_i)$ by $\epsilon$. The change in optimal parameters is:

\begin{equation}
\frac{d\theta^*}{d\epsilon_i} = -H^{-1} \nabla_\theta \ell(f_\theta(x_i), y_i)
\end{equation}

where $H = \nabla^2_\theta \mathcal{L}(\theta^*)$ is the Hessian of the training loss. The corresponding change in prediction at test point $x$ is:

\begin{equation}
\frac{df_\theta(x)}{d\epsilon_i} = \nabla_\theta f_\theta(x)^\top H^{-1} \nabla_\theta \ell(f_\theta(x_i), y_i)
\end{equation}

Bootstrap resampling induces random weights $w_i \sim \text{Multinomial}(n, 1/n)$ on training points. To first order, the bootstrap variance of predictions is:

\begin{equation}
\sigma^2_{\text{boot}}(x) \approx \nabla_\theta f_\theta(x)^\top H^{-1} \Sigma_g H^{-1} \nabla_\theta f_\theta(x)
\label{eq:sensitivity}
\end{equation}

where $\Sigma_g = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n g_i g_i^\top$ is the gradient covariance and $g_i = \nabla_\theta \ell(f_\theta(x_i), y_i)$.

Equation~\eqref{eq:sensitivity} reveals that prediction instability depends on three factors: (1) the sensitivity of predictions to parameters $\nabla_\theta f_\theta(x)$, (2) the curvature of the loss landscape $H^{-1}$, and (3) the variability of per-sample gradients $\Sigma_g$. Our variance penalty directly targets the compound effect of these factors by penalizing prediction disagreement under stochastic data perturbations.

\subsection{Bootstrap-Consistency Training Algorithm}

Direct optimization of bootstrap variance requires multiple complete training procedures, rendering it computationally prohibitive. We approximate this objective using Poisson-weighted losses within mini-batches, enabling efficient joint optimization.

Given a mini-batch $\mathcal{B} = \{(x_j, y_j)\}_{j=1}^B$, our algorithm maintains $K$ shadow copies of the model and proceeds as follows:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Shared-input forward pass}: All $K$ shadow models compute predictions on the \emph{same} mini-batch inputs:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{p}^{(k)} = f_{\theta^{(k)}}(\mathbf{x}) \in \mathbb{R}^B \quad \text{for } k = 1, \ldots, K
\end{equation}

\item \textbf{Poisson-weighted losses}: For each shadow model, simulate bootstrap resampling via independent Poisson(1) weights:
\begin{equation}
w_j^{(k)} \sim \text{Poisson}(1), \quad \mathcal{L}_{\text{sup}}^{(k)} = \frac{\sum_j w_j^{(k)} \ell(p_j^{(k)}, y_j)}{\sum_j w_j^{(k)}}
\end{equation}

The Poisson(1) weighting provides a computationally convenient approximation to multinomial bootstrap weights \citep{hanley2006poisson, chamandy2012estimating}. For full-dataset resampling, this approximation is asymptotically equivalent to the traditional bootstrap. However, applying Poisson weights within mini-batches is a heuristic extension: the mini-batch variance penalty serves as a stochastic proxy for full-dataset bootstrap variance, with approximation quality depending on factors discussed in Section~\ref{sec:theory_gaps}.

\item \textbf{Variance penalty}: Compute the variance of predictions across shadow models on identical inputs:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{var}} = \frac{1}{B}\sum_{j=1}^B \text{Var}_k[p_j^{(k)}]
\end{equation}

\item \textbf{Joint optimization}: Minimize the combined objective:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L} = \frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^K \mathcal{L}_{\text{sup}}^{(k)} + \lambda \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{var}}
\end{equation}
\end{enumerate}

The key insight is that all models predict on \emph{identical inputs}, making the variance penalty meaningful---it measures disagreement due to different bootstrap weighting, not different inputs. An alternative implementation might compute variance over predictions on \emph{different} resampled inputs, but this would conflate input variation with model variation, producing a noisy penalty signal.

\subsection{Implementation Considerations}

\textbf{Computational overhead}: Training requires $K$ forward passes per mini-batch, increasing computational cost by a factor of approximately $K$. Memory requirements scale linearly with $K$ due to the need to store multiple model copies.

\textbf{Inference}: At test time, predictions can be obtained from any single shadow model or their ensemble average. No additional computational cost is incurred during inference compared to standard training.

\textbf{Hyperparameter selection}: We fix $K=3$ across all experiments. For $\lambda$, we provide Pareto frontier analysis (Section~\ref{sec:pareto}) showing results across $\lambda \in \{0, 0.01, 0.02, 0.05, 0.1, 0.2, 0.5, 1.0\}$. We recommend $\lambda = 0.05$ as a default providing a good stability-accuracy trade-off.

\textbf{Connection to bagging}: Unlike standard bagging \citep{breiman1996bagging} which trains $K$ independent models on bootstrap samples, BCR jointly trains models while explicitly penalizing their disagreement. This encourages each shadow model to find solutions that are stable across bootstrap perturbations, rather than simply averaging diverse models.

\section{Experimental Setup}

\subsection{Datasets and Tasks}

We evaluate our approach on four tabular datasets spanning regression and binary classification:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Synthetic Regression}: 20-dimensional Gaussian features with linear target plus noise, $n=4000$
\item \textbf{California Housing}: Median house value prediction, 8 features, $n=20{,}640$
\item \textbf{Adult Income}: Binary income classification, 14 features (one-hot encoded), $n=48{,}842$
\item \textbf{German Credit Risk}: Binary credit risk classification, 20 features (one-hot encoded), $n=1000$
\end{itemize}

All datasets employ 75\%/25\% train/test splits (stratified for classification tasks) to ensure representative evaluation sets.

\subsection{Baseline Methods}

We compare BCR against seven baselines:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{ERM (Baseline)}: Standard empirical risk minimization with dropout.
\item \textbf{Weight Decay}: ERM with L2 regularization ($\lambda_{L2} = 10^{-2}$). Tests whether parameter regularization alone improves stability.
\item \textbf{SAM}: Sharpness-Aware Minimization \citep{foret2020sharpness} with $\rho = 0.05$. Tests whether flatter minima improve bootstrap stability.
\item \textbf{R-Drop}: Regularized dropout \citep{liang2021rdrop} with $\alpha = 0.5$. Tests whether consistency under dropout noise transfers to bootstrap stability.
\item \textbf{Bagging}: Standard ensemble of $K=3$ models trained independently on bootstrap samples \citep{breiman1996bagging}. Provides a direct comparison to ensemble methods.
\item \textbf{IFR (Diagonal)}: Influence Function Regularization using diagonal Fisher approximation. Based on the first-order sensitivity analysis (Equation~\ref{eq:sensitivity}), this method approximates the Hessian with a diagonal Fisher matrix and penalizes the estimated bootstrap variance $\sigma^2_{\text{boot}}(x)$ directly.
\item \textbf{IFR-KFAC}: Influence Function Regularization using Kronecker-Factored Approximate Curvature \citep{martens2015optimizing}. KFAC provides a better block-diagonal approximation to the Fisher by factoring each layer's Fisher as $F_l = A_l \otimes G_l$, where $A_l$ is the input activation covariance and $G_l$ is the output gradient covariance.
\end{itemize}

We note that baseline hyperparameters (weight decay strength, SAM $\rho$, R-Drop $\alpha$) were set to commonly recommended values rather than tuned for stability. A more comprehensive comparison would tune all methods on a validation set, though this would substantially increase computational cost.

\subsection{Model Architecture and Training}

We employ a standardized two-layer multilayer perceptron architecture across all experiments:
\begin{itemize}
\item Input layer to 64 hidden units with ReLU activation and dropout ($p=0.2$)
\item Hidden layer to 64 units with ReLU activation and dropout ($p=0.2$)
\item Output layer (1 unit for regression, 2 for classification)
\end{itemize}

Training configuration includes Adam optimization with learning rate $10^{-3}$, batch size 64, and 25 epochs for regression (20 for classification). We conduct 30 independent training runs per experimental condition to ensure statistical reliability.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

\textbf{Predictive performance}: We report test RMSE for regression tasks and classification accuracy for binary tasks.

\textbf{Bootstrap stability}: For each test input $x_i$, we compute the sample variance of predictions across 30 independent model fits and summarize stability as:

\begin{equation}
\text{StabilityRMSE} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{n_{\text{test}}}\sum_{i=1}^{n_{\text{test}}} \text{Var}_{\text{fit}}[\hat{f}(x_i)]}
\end{equation}

This metric quantifies prediction variability in the same units as the target variable, facilitating interpretation across tasks.

\textbf{Additional classification metrics}: For classification tasks, we additionally report:
\begin{itemize}
\item \emph{Probability stability}: Variance of predicted class probabilities (after softmax)
\item \emph{Flip rate}: Fraction of samples where at least one fit disagrees with the modal prediction
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}

Our approach differs fundamentally from existing stability methods in directly targeting prediction variance under data resampling rather than indirect proxies.

\textbf{Sharpness-based methods} such as Sharpness-Aware Minimization \citep{foret2020sharpness} and Entropy-SGD \citep{chaudhari2019entropy} seek flatter loss surfaces under the hypothesis that such minima correspond to more stable predictions. Our experiments show that SAM provides inconsistent stability improvements (0--11\% across datasets), suggesting that weight-space flatness is an imperfect proxy for bootstrap stability.

\textbf{Stochastic consistency methods} like R-Drop \citep{liang2021rdrop} enforce agreement between predictions under different dropout masks, addressing network stochasticity but not data resampling variance. R-Drop shows variable effectiveness, providing up to 17\% stability improvement on some datasets but occasionally increasing instability on others.

\textbf{Bagging and ensembles} \citep{breiman1996bagging} reduce variance through model averaging. Standard bagging with $K=3$ models provides competitive stability improvements and achieves the best stability on California Housing in our experiments. BCR outperforms bagging on classification tasks (Adult, German Credit) but not on California Housing regression, suggesting a pattern: BCR may provide greater benefits when prediction variance stems from model specification uncertainty (which regularization can address) rather than irreducible noise in the target variable (which it cannot). California Housing's continuous target with substantial measurement noise may favor bagging's averaging approach over BCR's attempt to stabilize individual models.

\textbf{Teacher-student approaches} including Mean Teacher \citep{tarvainen2017mean} stabilize training dynamics through exponential moving averages of model weights. While effective for reducing optimization noise, these methods do not explicitly address variability under data resampling.

\textbf{Distributionally robust optimization} methods such as $\chi^2$-DRO \citep{duchi2021learning} penalize loss variance across data subsets. However, loss variance does not directly correspond to prediction variance---models may achieve similar loss values while producing substantially different predictions.

\textbf{Influence functions} \citep{koh2017understanding} provide a theoretical framework for understanding prediction sensitivity to training data. Our first-order analysis (Equation~\ref{eq:sensitivity}) builds on this framework to explain why direct variance penalties are well-motivated.

\section{Discussion and Limitations}

\subsection{What BCR Stabilizes}

It is important to clarify exactly what BCR targets. The estimand is \emph{bootstrap prediction instability}---the variance of predictions at a given input across models trained on bootstrap resamples of the training data. This is a proxy for \emph{refit variance}, which practitioners encounter when retraining models on new data samples from the same distribution.

BCR does not target:
\begin{itemize}
\item \emph{Epistemic uncertainty}: BCR reduces variance, not bias, and does not provide calibrated uncertainty estimates.
\item \emph{Optimization noise}: Random seed variability within a single dataset is not the target (though BCR may reduce this as a side effect).
\item \emph{Distribution shift}: BCR does not address out-of-distribution robustness.
\end{itemize}

For classification, BCR stabilizes all levels of the prediction pipeline: logits, probabilities, and hard labels. The flip rate reduction (Table~\ref{tab:classification}) is particularly relevant for deployment scenarios where label consistency across model versions is critical.

\subsection{Computational Considerations and Scalability}

The primary limitation of our approach is computational overhead. Training $K$ shadow models increases memory requirements by a factor of $K$ and training time by approximately $2$--$3\times$ due to additional forward passes and the variance penalty computation. We note that BCR and standard bagging have similar computational requirements: both train $K$ models, though BCR couples them through the variance penalty while bagging trains them independently. The practical difference is that BCR requires all $K$ models in memory simultaneously for the variance penalty, while bagging can train models sequentially.

\textbf{Scope and scalability:} Our experiments focus on tabular data with shallow networks (2-layer MLPs with $\sim$9K parameters) and moderate dataset sizes ($n = 1{,}000$ to $48{,}000$). We do not evaluate BCR on deep networks (ResNets, Transformers), high-dimensional inputs (images, text), or large-scale datasets. Scaling BCR to such settings raises additional challenges: memory requirements for $K$ copies of a large model may be prohibitive, and the mini-batch Poisson approximation quality may degrade differently in high-capacity models. Extending BCR to modern deep learning architectures is an important direction for future work.

\subsection{Influence Function Regularization: Single-Model Alternative}

We also evaluate Influence Function Regularization (IFR) as a single-model alternative to BCR. The first-order sensitivity analysis (Equation~\ref{eq:sensitivity}) suggests that bootstrap variance can be approximated as $\sigma^2_{\text{boot}}(x) \approx J_x^\top H^{-1} \Sigma_g H^{-1} J_x$, where $J_x = \nabla_\theta f_\theta(x)$ is the prediction Jacobian, $H$ is the Hessian, and $\Sigma_g$ is the gradient covariance. IFR directly penalizes this estimated variance during training.

We implement two variants: (1) \emph{IFR-Diagonal}, which approximates $H$ with a diagonal Fisher matrix, and (2) \emph{IFR-KFAC}, which uses Kronecker-Factored Approximate Curvature \citep{martens2015optimizing} for a block-diagonal approximation that captures within-layer parameter correlations.

Our experiments reveal that both IFR variants provide minimal stability improvement. Diagonal IFR achieves $<$2\% improvement across datasets, while IFR-KFAC performs comparably---showing marginal improvement on one dataset (California Housing: 7\% better than diagonal) but no improvement on others.

Several factors may explain IFR's limited effectiveness:
\begin{itemize}
\item \emph{First-order approximation limitations}: The linear approximation $\sigma^2_{\text{boot}}(x) \approx J^\top H^{-1} \Sigma_g H^{-1} J$ may be insufficient to capture the nonlinear relationship between data perturbations and prediction changes during neural network training.
\item \emph{Objective mismatch}: IFR penalizes the \emph{estimated} influence-based variance at each training step, but this may not correspond to minimizing \emph{actual} bootstrap variance at convergence. The penalty changes the loss landscape in ways the influence framework does not predict.
\item \emph{Hessian approximation artifacts}: While KFAC provides no meaningful improvement over diagonal approximations (suggesting the Hessian approximation is not the bottleneck), both approximations may still introduce optimization artifacts when used as regularization penalties.
\end{itemize}

The key insight from our experiments is that BCR's joint training of multiple models under different bootstrap weights provides a more direct optimization signal than single-model influence approximations. By actually training multiple models with different Poisson weights and penalizing their disagreement, BCR sidesteps the need for accurate influence function estimation.

\subsection{Theoretical Gaps}
\label{sec:theory_gaps}

Our first-order sensitivity analysis (Equation~\ref{eq:sensitivity}) provides intuition for why BCR works but does not constitute a formal guarantee. The mini-batch Poisson approximation in particular requires careful consideration: while Poisson(1) weights are asymptotically equivalent to multinomial bootstrap weights for full-dataset resampling \citep{hanley2006poisson}, our method applies these weights within mini-batches during SGD training. This is a heuristic extension---the mini-batch variance penalty provides a stochastic estimate of the variance we would observe under full-dataset retraining, but the relationship between these quantities depends on training dynamics in ways we do not formally characterize.

Key open questions include:
\begin{itemize}
\item Under what conditions does mini-batch Poisson weighting accurately approximate full-dataset bootstrap variance? Batch size likely plays a critical role: larger batches should provide better approximations, but we test only $B=64$.
\item How does the approximation quality depend on the number of shadow models $K$ and the stage of training? We test only $K=3$; different values may offer different stability-compute trade-offs.
\item Can we derive optimal $\lambda$ as a function of dataset characteristics (sample size, dimensionality, signal-to-noise ratio)?
\item How sensitive is BCR to other hyperparameters (learning rate, network depth/width)? Our experiments use a fixed architecture and training configuration; broader hyperparameter sensitivity analysis would strengthen the empirical findings.
\end{itemize}

We emphasize that our empirical validation (Section~\ref{sec:pareto}) demonstrates that the mini-batch surrogate \emph{does} reduce actual full-retraining instability, even though we lack formal guarantees about when or why this approximation works well.

\subsection{Statistical Methodology}

We report results across $R=30$ independent training runs per method. For stability metrics, we compute bootstrap confidence intervals by resampling over the $R$ runs: for each of $B=2000$ bootstrap samples, we draw $R$ runs with replacement and recompute the stability RMSE, yielding a distribution from which we extract the standard error and 95\% confidence interval. This approach accounts for the fact that stability is estimated from the cross-run variance structure.

For pairwise method comparisons, we use a paired bootstrap test: we resample the same run indices for both methods, compute the difference in stability, and assess whether the 95\% CI for the difference excludes zero. We also report Cohen's $d$ effect sizes using the pooled standard deviation of bootstrap stability estimates.

This statistical framework allows us to distinguish:
\begin{itemize}
\item \emph{Significant improvements}: BCR vs baseline shows significant reductions on German Credit ($p<0.01$, $d>0.8$) and Adult Income ($p<0.01$, $d>0.5$).
\item \emph{Comparable methods}: BCR vs bagging on California Housing and Synthetic shows differences not statistically distinguishable from zero ($p>0.05$).
\item \emph{Trade-off cases}: Weight decay achieves better logit stability than BCR on Adult ($p<0.01$), suggesting different methods excel in different contexts.
\end{itemize}

All stability estimates in Table~\ref{tab:main_results} include bootstrap standard errors in parentheses.

\subsection{When BCR Helps Most}

Our ablation studies (Table~\ref{tab:ablation}) suggest BCR provides the largest benefits in high-variance settings caused by model specification issues: underdetermined problems (66\% reduction) and overcapacity (56\% reduction). However, BCR provides minimal benefit under label noise (6\% reduction), indicating that variance caused by irreducible noise in the labels cannot be addressed through regularization.

More broadly, our main results show that BCR's relative advantage is dataset-dependent:
\begin{itemize}
\item \emph{BCR vs.\ bagging}: BCR outperforms bagging on classification tasks (Adult: 0.15 vs 0.22; German: 0.06 vs 0.08 logit stability) but loses on California Housing regression (0.07 vs 0.05 stability RMSE). We hypothesize that BCR excels when variance stems from model specification uncertainty---the penalty encourages models to find stable solutions across bootstrap perturbations. Bagging may be preferred when variance stems primarily from irreducible target noise, where averaging diverse predictions is more effective than trying to stabilize individual models.
\item \emph{BCR vs.\ weight decay}: On Adult Income, weight decay achieves dramatically better logit stability (0.04 vs BCR's 0.15). This occurs because weight decay directly constrains weight magnitudes, producing smaller logits and hence lower logit variance. However, this aggressive shrinkage may affect calibration---BCR's stability improvements operate through a different mechanism that does not uniformly shrink predictions.
\end{itemize}

Practitioners should consider: (1) whether variance is primarily reducible (model uncertainty) or irreducible (target noise), (2) whether logit-level stability or probability/label stability is the priority, and (3) computational constraints.

\section{Conclusion}

We have presented bootstrap-consistency regularization (BCR), a training procedure that directly minimizes prediction instability under data resampling. Our key contributions include: (1) clearly defining bootstrap prediction instability as the target estimand, (2) a tractable training algorithm using Poisson-weighted losses to simulate bootstrap perturbations, (3) first-order sensitivity analysis justifying why direct variance penalties target the right quantity, and (4) empirical evaluation demonstrating 22--50\% reductions in instability across four datasets.

The main question we address is: \emph{does the stochastic training surrogate actually reduce full-retraining instability?} Our experiments confirm that it does, though effectiveness varies by dataset and problem structure. BCR excels on classification tasks and high-variance settings (underdetermined problems, overcapacity), while simpler methods like bagging or weight decay may suffice for some regression tasks or when label noise is present.

The key insight is that stability under data resampling benefits from directly penalizing prediction disagreement across bootstrap perturbations on \emph{identical inputs}, rather than relying on indirect proxies. Our evaluation of Influence Function Regularization (IFR) as a single-model alternative shows that first-order approximations of bootstrap variance provide essentially no benefit: both diagonal IFR and KFAC-based IFR achieve $<$5\% stability improvement, with KFAC providing no meaningful advantage over the simpler diagonal approximation. While multiple factors may explain IFR's limited effectiveness (including possible objective mismatch between training-time penalty and converged variance), the joint training of multiple models under different bootstrap weights appears to provide a more direct optimization signal than single-model influence approximations.

While computationally more demanding than single-model training, BCR provides a principled approach to bootstrap prediction instability that is particularly effective on classification tasks and high-variance settings, though simpler methods may suffice in other contexts.

Future research directions include improving single-model approximations through higher-order influence expansions or learned curvature models, establishing theoretical guarantees for the mini-batch Poisson approximation, extending to other architectures (transformers, CNNs), combining BCR with other regularization techniques, and conducting comprehensive hyperparameter sensitivity analysis (varying $K$, batch size, and network architecture) to understand when BCR provides the largest benefits.
